# The Objection Dance Playbook — Eli Wilde

## Core Principle
An objection is not a "no." It's a "not yet." Every objection is a signal that the prospect needs more certainty in one of three areas: the offer, you, or themselves. Your job is to dance with the objection — not fight it.

## The Dance vs. The Fight

| The Fight (Old Way) | The Dance (Wilde Way) |
|---|---|
| Argue against the objection | Agree with their feeling |
| Push harder | Pull back and redirect |
| Make them wrong | Make them understood |
| Creates resistance | Creates openness |
| Win/lose dynamic | Both move forward together |

## The 7 Most Common Objections

### 1. "I need to think about it"

**What it really means:** Something doesn't feel resolved. They can't articulate what.

**The Dance:**
"Of course — this is a big decision and I respect that. Can I ask you something? What specifically do you want to think about? Is it the program itself, whether it's right timing, or something else?"

Then address whatever they surface. Often they don't actually know what they need to think about — helping them name it resolves it.

### 2. "It's too expensive" / "I can't afford it"

**What it really means:** The perceived value doesn't justify the price yet.

**The Dance:**
"I hear you. And I'd never want you to invest in something that doesn't make sense financially. Let me ask — if the investment was $0, would you move forward today?"

If yes: "So it's not about whether you want this — it's about whether the ROI makes sense. Let's talk about what this is actually worth to you in revenue/time/stress over the next 12 months."

If no: There's another hidden objection. Surface it.

### 3. "I need to talk to my partner/spouse"

**What it really means:** Either genuine (respect it) or they're not sold enough to advocate for it.

**The Dance:**
"Absolutely — big decisions should be made together. Let me ask: if it were entirely your decision, would you move forward?"

If yes: "Great. What would your partner need to know to feel as good about this as you do? Let me help you explain it — or I'm happy to jump on a quick call with both of you."

If no: There's another objection. The partner is the shield.

### 4. "Now's not the right time"

**What it really means:** They're afraid of change, or they don't feel the urgency.

**The Dance:**
"I totally get that. When do you think the right time would be? ... And what changes between now and then?"

Then: "Here's what I've noticed — the 'right time' rarely announces itself. The people who get the best results are the ones who start when it feels slightly uncomfortable. What if the right time is right now, and the discomfort is just the signal that you're ready for the next level?"

### 5. "I've tried something like this before and it didn't work"

**What it really means:** They're protecting themselves from disappointment.

**The Dance:**
"I appreciate you sharing that — it takes courage to try again after a bad experience. Can I ask what specifically didn't work about it?"

Listen. Then: "That makes total sense. Here's what's different about this approach: [specific differentiator]. But more importantly — you learned from that experience. You know now what to look for and what to avoid. That actually makes you the ideal person for this."

### 6. "I need to do more research"

**What it really means:** They don't have enough information, or they're avoiding a decision.

**The Dance:**
"Smart — I'd do the same thing. What specifically are you looking to find out? I might be able to answer it right now and save you some time."

Address each concern directly. Often "research" is code for "I need more certainty" — provide it in the conversation.

### 7. "Can you send me more information?"

**What it really means:** They want to disengage without saying no.

**The Dance:**
"Absolutely — what specifically would be most helpful for me to send? I want to make sure I give you exactly what you need, not a generic brochure."

This forces specificity. If they can't name what they want, it's not about information — it's about certainty.

## The Post-Objection Check

After every objection dance:
"Does that make sense?"
"How do you feel about that?"
"Is there anything else holding you back?"

Keep going until all objections are resolved or until it's genuinely not a fit.

## The Walk-Away Close

Sometimes the most powerful move is to give permission to say no:
"Look — I only want you to do this if it feels absolutely right. If there's any part of you that isn't certain, let's hold off. I'd rather you come back when you're ready than move forward with doubt."

**Why it works:** Removing pressure creates safety. Safety creates certainty. Certainty creates the close.

## Key Insight
"Every objection is a gift — it's telling you exactly where the certainty gap is. Don't be afraid of objections. Welcome them. Dance with them. The close lives on the other side of the last objection."
