# Tactical Empathy & Negotiation — Chris Voss

## Core Principle
Negotiation is not about winning or compromise. It's about discovering information and making the other side feel understood. When people feel heard, they become open. Tactical empathy is the deliberate use of emotional intelligence to influence outcomes.

## The Core Techniques

### 1. Mirroring
Repeat the last 1–3 words (or critical words) of what the other person just said. Then go silent.

- Triggers the other person to elaborate
- Makes them feel heard without you doing any work
- Creates rapport unconsciously

**Example:**
- Prospect: "We're really struggling with our conversion rates."
- You: "Your conversion rates?"
- Prospect: (elaborates with 3x more detail)

### 2. Labeling
Name the emotion you sense. Start with "It seems like..." or "It sounds like..."

- Validates their feelings without agreeing or disagreeing
- Diffuses negative emotions instantly
- Surfaces hidden concerns

**Example:**
- "It seems like you're frustrated with solutions that overpromise."
- "It sounds like timing is a real concern for you."

### 3. Calibrated Questions
Open-ended questions starting with "What" or "How" that guide the conversation while making the other person feel in control.

- "How am I supposed to do that?" (gentle pushback)
- "What's the biggest challenge you're facing with this?"
- "How would you like to proceed?"
- "What happens if you don't solve this?"

**Never ask "Why"** — it triggers defensiveness.

### 4. The Accusation Audit
List every negative thing the other person might think about you, your offer, or the situation — before they say it. Defuse it upfront.

**Example:**
"You're probably thinking this sounds too good to be true. You might be wondering if this is just another program that overpromises. You may even be thinking, 'Why should I trust this person?' — and those are all fair concerns."

This takes the sting out of every objection before it arises.

### 5. "No"-Oriented Questions
People feel safe saying "no." Design questions where "no" moves the conversation forward.

- "Would it be a terrible idea if...?"
- "Is it a bad time to talk about this?"
- "Have you given up on solving this?"

**The psychology:** "No" gives people a sense of control. "Yes" makes people feel trapped.

### 6. "That's Right" (Not "You're Right")
The goal of every negotiation is to get the other person to say "that's right." This means they feel fully understood.

- Summarize their situation so completely they say "that's right"
- "That's right" = breakthrough. "You're right" = they want you to go away.

**How to get there:**
1. Label their emotions
2. Paraphrase their situation
3. Summarize: emotions + situation + stakes
4. Wait for "that's right"

### 7. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice
Slow down. Lower your voice. Speak calmly and deliberately.

- Creates authority without aggression
- Calms the emotional temperature of any conversation
- Signals confidence and control

## Application to Sales

| Sales Situation | Voss Technique |
|---|---|
| Discovery call opening | Accusation Audit |
| Understanding their pain | Mirroring + Calibrated Questions |
| Objection handling | Labeling + "No"-oriented questions |
| Price resistance | "How am I supposed to..." + Mirroring |
| Getting commitment | Aim for "That's Right" moment |
| Stalled deal | "Have you given up on...?" |

## Key Insight
"He who has learned to disagree without being disagreeable has discovered the most valuable secret of negotiation. Empathy is not about being nice — it's about being understood."
