# Never Split the Difference — Chris Voss

## Core Principle
Never split the difference. A compromise where both sides lose something is not a good deal — it's a lazy deal. The goal of negotiation is to discover what the other side really wants and find creative ways to give it to them while getting what you need.

## The Negotiation Toolkit

### 1. The Black Swan Theory
In every negotiation, there are unknown unknowns — pieces of information that, if discovered, would change everything. Your job is to find the Black Swans.

**How to find them:**
- Ask open-ended questions
- Listen for what they DON'T say
- Watch for emotional shifts
- Look for contradictions
- The most important information is often shared "off the record" or in casual moments

### 2. The FBI's Behavioral Change Stairway

1. **Active Listening** — Really hear what they're saying (and not saying)
2. **Empathy** — Demonstrate understanding of their world
3. **Rapport** — Build trust through consistent empathetic engagement
4. **Influence** — Once they feel understood, they're open to your ideas
5. **Behavioral Change** — They act differently (agree, concede, collaborate)

You cannot skip steps. Most people jump straight to #4 (influence) and wonder why it doesn't work.

### 3. Bending Reality

**Loss Aversion:** People are 2x more motivated to avoid loss than to achieve gain.

**Application:**
- Frame your proposal in terms of what they'll LOSE by not agreeing
- "If we don't move forward by Friday, the window on this opportunity closes"
- "Without this, you'll continue spending $X/month on the problem"

**Anchoring:**
- Set an extreme anchor first (your ideal outcome)
- Let them negotiate down to what you actually want
- "I was thinking something in the range of [high anchor]" → they counter → you "concede" to your real target

### 4. The Ackerman Bargaining System

A systematic approach to negotiation:

1. Set your target price (what you actually want)
2. Set your first offer at 65% of target
3. Calculate three raises: 85%, 95%, 100%
4. Use empathy and calibrated questions between each raise
5. On your final number, use a non-round number ($37,263 instead of $37,000)
6. On the final offer, throw in a non-monetary item

**Why it works:** Demonstrates that you're making genuine concessions while still reaching your target.

### 5. Dealing with "Yes"

Three types of "yes":
- **Counterfeit** — "Yes" to get you to go away. Body language says otherwise.
- **Confirmation** — "Yes" as in "I hear you." Non-committal.
- **Commitment** — "Yes" with action behind it. The only one that matters.

**How to test:** After getting a "yes," ask: "What happens next?" If they can't articulate next steps, it's not a commitment yes.

### 6. The Power of "How"

"How am I supposed to do that?" is the most powerful phrase in negotiation.

- It says "no" without saying no
- It makes them solve the problem for you
- It reveals their constraints
- It creates empathy for your position

**Other powerful "how" questions:**
- "How would you like me to proceed?"
- "How does this work on your end?"
- "How can we make this work for both of us?"

## Application to Business Sales

### Discovery Calls
- Use mirroring to get them talking
- Label emotions: "It sounds like you're frustrated with..."
- Calibrated questions: "What's the biggest challenge you're facing?"
- Aim for "That's right" before presenting your solution

### Price Negotiation
- Never negotiate against yourself
- If they push on price: "How am I supposed to do that and still deliver the result you want?"
- Anchor high, concede slowly
- Non-round numbers communicate precision

### Difficult Clients
- Accusation audit upfront: "You might feel like I haven't been responsive enough..."
- Label the emotion before addressing the issue
- "What would it take for you to feel good about this?"

### Closing
- "It seems like you're ready to move forward" (label)
- "What would need to happen for you to feel comfortable with this decision?" (calibrated question)
- Silence after presenting the offer. Let them process.

## Key Insight
"Negotiation is not about getting your way. It's about gathering information, understanding the other side's worldview, and finding a path where both sides feel heard. When people feel understood, they become flexible. When they feel pressured, they become rigid."
