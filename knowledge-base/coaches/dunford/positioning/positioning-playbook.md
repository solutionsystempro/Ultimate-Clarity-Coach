# The Positioning Playbook — April Dunford

## Core Principle
Positioning is context setting. It defines how your offer is the best in the world at providing something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about. Weak positioning makes everything harder — marketing, sales, product development. Strong positioning makes everything easier.

## The 10-Step Positioning Process

### Step 1: Understand Who Loves Your Product
Start with your best customers — not your average ones.
- Who are your happiest, most successful clients?
- What do they have in common?
- Why did they choose you over alternatives?

### Step 2: List Your Competitive Alternatives
What would your best customers do if your product/offer didn't exist?
- Direct competitors
- DIY / manual processes
- Hiring someone
- Using a different type of solution
- Doing nothing

### Step 3: Identify Your Unique Attributes
What do you have or do that the alternatives can't match?
- Features, methodology, expertise
- Speed, depth, format
- Community, network, access
- Technology, IP, process

### Step 4: Map Attributes to Value Themes
Translate each attribute into a customer benefit:
- "We have [attribute]" → "Which means you get [value]"
- Group similar values into themes (2–3 themes max)

### Step 5: Find Your Best-Fit Customers
Who cares the MOST about this specific value?
- Not demographics — buying characteristics
- What makes someone an ideal fit vs. a poor fit?
- What signals indicate someone is in your sweet spot?

### Step 6: Choose Your Market Category
Three options:

**Existing Category (Head-to-Head)**
- "We're a [category] that does [unique value]"
- Works when: you have clear, defensible differentiation
- Risk: compared directly to established players

**Subcategory (Niche)**
- "We're a [category] specifically for [niche]"
- Works when: one segment values your uniqueness far more than others
- Advantage: inherit category understanding, own the niche

**New Category (Category Creation)**
- "We've created [new category name]"
- Works when: nothing existing describes what you do
- Risk: you must educate the market (expensive and slow)
- Reward: if you win, you own the category

### Step 7: Layer on Trends (Optional)
Connect your positioning to a relevant market trend:
- "AI-powered [category]"
- "Built for the remote-first economy"
- Only if the trend genuinely applies — never force it

### Step 8: Craft Your Positioning Statement
**For** [best-fit customer]
**Who** [key need/pain point]
**Unlike** [competitive alternative]
**Our** [product/offer]
**Delivers** [unique value]
**Because** [unique attributes/proof]

### Step 9: Test with Real Customers
- Share the positioning with 5–10 best-fit customers
- Do they immediately "get it"?
- Does it match how they think about your value?
- Adjust based on their language (use their words, not yours)

### Step 10: Align Everything
Once positioning is set, align:
- Website messaging and copy
- Sales pitch and demo flow
- Marketing campaigns and content
- Product roadmap priorities
- Hiring and team language

## Common Positioning Mistakes

1. **Positioning for everyone** — Weak positioning = generic value prop = lost in the noise
2. **Positioning on features** — Customers buy outcomes, not features
3. **Copying competitor positioning** — You fight on their terms, on their turf
4. **Positioning based on what you WISH were true** — Must be provably, demonstrably true today
5. **Setting and forgetting** — Positioning should evolve as your market, product, and customers evolve

## The "Obviously Awesome" Test

After defining your positioning, answer:
- Is it immediately clear what makes you different?
- Would your best customer say "YES, that's exactly why I chose you"?
- Can a new salesperson explain your value in 30 seconds?
- Does your positioning make your marketing easier to write?

If any answer is "no" — keep iterating.

## Key Insight
"Positioning is not a tagline, a brand, or a marketing campaign. It's the strategic foundation that everything else is built on. Get it right, and your offer becomes 'obviously awesome.' Get it wrong, and no amount of marketing spend can fix it."
