# Obviously Awesome Positioning — April Dunford

## Core Principle
Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best in the world at providing something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about. If your positioning is weak, everything else — marketing, sales, product — has to work harder.

## The 5 Components of Positioning

### 1. Competitive Alternatives
What would customers do if your solution didn't exist?

- Not just direct competitors — include "do nothing," spreadsheets, hiring someone, etc.
- Understanding the real alternative reveals your real value
- Ask: "What were you doing before you found us?"

### 2. Unique Attributes
What do you have that the alternatives don't?

- Features, capabilities, expertise, approach, speed, methodology
- Must be provably different — not "better service" but specific, tangible differences
- List everything, then rank by importance to your best customers

### 3. Value (and Proof)
What value do those unique attributes deliver?

- Translate features into outcomes: "We have X, which means you get Y"
- Back up with proof: case studies, data, testimonials
- The value must matter deeply to your target segment

### 4. Target Customer Characteristics
Who cares the most about the value you deliver?

- Not demographics — buying characteristics
- What makes someone a "best-fit" customer?
- Signals: industry, company size, tech stack, pain level, urgency, budget
- The tighter your focus, the stronger your positioning

### 5. Market Category
What market do you position yourself in?

- Category triggers expectations — use this strategically
- Options:
  - **Existing category:** Compete head-on (need clear differentiation)
  - **Subcategory:** "CRM for real estate agents" — inherit category, narrow focus
  - **New category:** Create your own (high risk, high reward, requires education)

## The Positioning Process

### Step 1: List your competitive alternatives
### Step 2: Map your unique attributes against each alternative
### Step 3: Translate attributes into value themes
### Step 4: Identify the customers who care most about that value
### Step 5: Choose the market category that makes your value obvious

## Positioning Statement Template

**For** [target customer]
**Who** [key need/pain]
**Unlike** [competitive alternative]
**Our** [product/offer]
**Delivers** [unique value]
**Because of** [unique attributes/proof]

## Common Positioning Mistakes

1. **Trying to appeal to everyone** — Weak positioning = weak everything
2. **Positioning on features, not value** — Customers buy outcomes
3. **Ignoring the competitive alternative** — You're always compared to something
4. **Copying competitor positioning** — You end up fighting on their terms
5. **Not validating with real customers** — Internal assumptions ≠ market reality

## Key Insight
"You can have the greatest product in the world, but if customers can't figure out what it is and why it matters, you'll lose to an inferior product with better positioning."
